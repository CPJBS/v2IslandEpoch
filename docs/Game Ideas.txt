Designing a resource system that spans from a prehistoric start to a spacefaring finale requires carefully staged progression. The game is envisioned as a semi-idle city-builder: players gradually advance through 10 epochs, each introducing new mechanics and complexity, with the ultimate objective of building a rocket to escape the planet. This multi-era structure is similar to progression in games like Anno 1800 or idle civilization simulators – early stages are simple and tutorial-like, while later stages layer on complexity  . Crucially, the final “rocket” serves as a long-term goal and resource sink that motivates the entire journey. Successful designs often promise the end goal early (e.g. hinting at the rocket from the start) to give players a clear direction . Each epoch should feel like a milestone, rewarding the player with new capabilities and tougher challenges in resource management. By the end, the player will have transformed a primitive archipelago into an industrialized, space-capable civilization – all while balancing an idle economy with logistics planning gameplay.

Core Resource and Production Systems

To maintain clarity across so many resources and buildings, everything should serve a distinct role in the economy. In successful city/idle builders, every game element is either a source, converter, sink, storage, transport, or modifier. If something doesn’t fit one of these roles, it tends to be unnecessary UI noise. For example:
	•	Sources (e.g. foragers, mines, farms) generate base resources over time, essentially converting time (or worker effort) into Tier-1 goods.
	•	Converters (smelters, workshops, factories) consume inputs to produce higher-tier outputs, forming processing chains and potential bottlenecks.
	•	Sinks (construction projects, upkeep, research, rocket parts) consume resources, removing them from the economy to gate progress and provide goals. The rocket and its sub-components will be major sinks in Epoch 10.
	•	Storage/Capacitors (warehouses, silos) define how much can be accumulated – effectively capping offline idle gains and forcing the player to expand capacity or logistics when full. Tying offline gains to storage ensures players can’t hoard infinite resources passively .
	•	Transportation (ships and shipping lanes in this game) adds friction by requiring time and planning to move goods between islands. This prevents the game from being a trivial “one big inventory” and makes island layout and distances matter in production efficiency.
	•	Modifiers (power generators, efficiency boosters, rare bonuses) multiply or tweak output and add strategic choices. For instance, electricity in later epochs boosts production but introduces a power budget that the player must manage as another constraint.

By clearly defining each building or mechanic under these categories, we keep the economic logic “ruthlessly clear”. This approach echoes classic factory sims like Factorio, where each machine has a clear input-output role, and any item with no purpose is quickly obsoleted or avoided. It also aligns with idle game design principles where simplicity and clarity are key to prevent player overload .

Resource Complexity and Balance Across Eras

One of the biggest challenges in a 10-epoch game is managing complexity creep – by the late game you could have dozens of resources and products (the initial spec mentioned ~47 resources). To keep this manageable, we should use progressive disclosure and resource combining strategies: in early game, group resources into broad categories, then gradually refine them into specific items as needed. For example, we might start with a generic “Food” resource in E1, which covers berries or basic meals. In E2, when farming is introduced, “Food” can branch into Bread as a new, more efficient food – but the UI could still show a single food counter if needed, with Bread providing a bonus or replacing berries. Similarly, early “Fuel” might just be a generic measure (burnable material), whether it comes from wood or charcoal; only in later epochs do we differentiate coal, oil, etc., when those become relevant. Some strategy games use this tactic of resource pooling to reduce cognitive load – for instance, early stages of Civilization or Rise of Nations hide advanced resources until the appropriate era, and idle games like To the Stars Idle keep the resource list manageable until you’ve unlocked the next set  . By gating visibility this way, a player in Epoch 2 might only see 5–6 resource types, even if 40 exist in the data for Epoch 10. New resources are revealed with purpose and context, so the player never has to learn more than a few at once. This aligns with Anno 1800’s design where each population tier adds a few new goods demands, rather than dumping all goods on the player upfront .

Balancing the resource ratios in production chains is another critical aspect. A smooth economy requires that inputs and outputs align without frequent stalls or gluts. A good rule of thumb is to ensure the slowest step in a chain defines the throughput, and every upstream producer supplies slightly more (~10% extra) than its downstream consumer needs . This way, minor inefficiencies or travel delays won’t starve the chain. For instance, if a bakery consumes 1 flour per minute, your mills should produce about 1.1 flour per minute or more. If you only produce exactly 1, any hiccup means the bakery idles – an experience we want to minimize unless it’s the player’s strategic choice to under-supply. Many factory-building games teach this implicitly: players learn to build a surplus of base ingredients to keep advanced production flowing. We can assist by providing ratio guidelines in the UI (e.g. highlight if a furnace isn’t getting enough ore per minute).

Co-products and byproducts deserve special attention in balance. If a production building yields two outputs (say Product A and byproduct B), both outputs should have valuable uses within a reasonable timeframe, or else one becomes “trash” that clutters storage. A classic example is oil refining in Factorio: basic oil processing used to output heavy oil, light oil, and petroleum gas; new players often didn’t have uses for heavy oil immediately and their refineries would clog and stop . The solution was to unlock more recipes (like cracking heavy oil into lighter forms) or provide early uses (lubricant, fuel) so that every output matters. In our design, we will introduce co-products only when we have at least two genuine sinks for them. For instance, if an oil refinery produces Fuel and Plastics, we ensure that Plastics are needed for, say, electronics or building materials in the next epoch and fuel is continuously needed for power or transport. We could also allow players some control, such as a ratio slider or priority setting at the refinery, to tune outputs based on what they currently need. This prevents frustration and lets advanced players optimize (perhaps at the cost of some efficiency). The game Anno 1800 handles this by giving certain buildings (like chemical plants) multiple potential recipes, and the player chooses which output to focus on; we can keep it simpler with just a slider for co-product ratio. The key is to avoid scenarios where players are forced to “dump” excess goods with no benefit – that’s unfun micromanagement. If a surplus is unavoidable, we should provide a relief valve (like trading away excess or converting it into something useful, e.g. scrap or energy).

We also have to prevent resource obsolescence from making earlier islands/routes irrelevant. As we introduce new materials, earlier ones might decline in importance (for example, wood is less needed once coal arrives for fuel). It’s good design to reuse older resources in new recipes so they stay relevant. Perhaps wood gets processed into charcoal for steelmaking, or stone is always needed for construction even in late game (albeit in smaller relative quantities). This way, the player’s early infrastructure isn’t wasted; instead, it feeds into the next tiers. However, we can retire certain items if they become redundant and replace them with the upgraded form in a streamlined way. For example, early “Building Materials” could start as timber, then bricks, then steel frame, etc., but the game can treat them under one category “Construction materials” that upgrades over epochs. Old stockpiles might even auto-convert at some ratio when moving to the next age (to prevent hoarding exploits and reduce clutter). This concept is used in some idle civ games where your “tools” resource upgrades from stone tools to iron tools, etc., rather than keeping separate counts.

Cost scaling and pacing must be tuned per epoch so the game remains challenging but not grindy. Typically, building costs and research costs grow exponentially with a factor that increases in later epochs. Early on, an exponent around 1.15 per level is fine (upgrades get ~15% more expensive each step) . Mid-game could ease to ~1.10 if we want a gentler curve while introducing new mechanics, and endgame might spike to 1.2 or more for “wonder” projects like the rocket. Many F2P and idle games use exponential cost curves to pace content – if it’s too steep (above 1.25), progress might feel frustratingly slow or force monetization . We’ll calibrate this so that active play and idle gains combined let a dedicated player reach the rocket in a satisfying timeframe (perhaps a few weeks of play).

We will also use soft caps rather than hard limits to make players plan instead of hitting brick walls. For example, rather than saying “you cannot produce more than X of item Y,” we say “to produce more, you need more storage or power.” Storage is a natural cap for idle gains – once full, production halts, encouraging the player to come back and spend resources or upgrade capacities. Workers (if we have a population system) could be another cap: you can only staff so many buildings without more housing. Power is a global cap in mid-late game: you can’t just run every machine at once if you exceed the grid capacity – you must build more power plants or improve efficiency. These caps make the player make decisions (“Should I invest in a new warehouse or build a second power plant first?”) rather than passively waiting. They are also forgiving in that the game doesn’t outright stop you from progressing; it signals you to redirect your efforts.

Finally, economy tuning will rely on data and possibly a consistent metric. A good approach is to define a hidden Reference Unit (RU), like “1 unit of basic production = X seconds of worker time,” and price everything in terms of that. This way we can ensure, say, 1 iron bar is worth 5 wood in terms of effort, or 1 unit of electronics is worth 100 wood, etc., giving us a baseline to compare disparate goods. It’s a bit like how Factorio or Satisfactory players compute everything in “crafting seconds” to balance factory ratios. Internally using such a metric (even if players don’t see it) ensures that each new epoch’s resources require proportionally more base resources/time, creating a smooth difficulty ramp. If Wheat → Flour → Bread has a value chain, we assign RU values that make sense (e.g. bread might be “worth” 3 wheat in effort, etc.). This also helps later if we add a prestige or rebirth mechanic – we can adjust RU yields by a percentage to speed up early-game on subsequent runs, for example.

Logistics and Transportation Challenges

Starting in Epoch 6, transportation becomes the linchpin of the game’s complexity. The introduction of multiple islands with unique resource profiles means the player must coordinate a inter-island trade network. This design is inspired by games like Anno 1800, which requires players to ferry goods between islands to satisfy all needs, and by transport management sims. One important design decision we’ve made is to abstract all on-island transport (no roads or trucks to micromanage) and focus purely on cross-island shipping. This keeps the logistical challenge centered on strategic routing rather than fiddling with internal logistics (which can become very complex and is a different kind of game – we don’t want a full Transport Tycoon on each island!). Instead, each island in our game is assumed to internally distribute goods (perhaps magically or with minor flavor animations), and the player’s job is to ensure the right goods get to the right islands via ships.

To make this interesting, distance and ship capacity must matter. In Epoch 6, the initial Skiff is intentionally limited – it might carry 50 units and move slowly, so shipping goods long distances is time-consuming. The travel time is likely modeled as (distance / speed) + fixed dock times for loading/unloading. Thus a far island might take, say, 5 minutes one-way for a Skiff plus 30 seconds at each port to load/unload. If the island needs 100 units of something per minute, a single Skiff clearly can’t handle that – prompting the player to build more boats or advance to better ships. Each subsequent epoch offers a ship upgrade: the Cutter in E7 (larger and a bit faster), Freighter in E8 (huge capacity), Container Ship in E9 (very large and faster). These larger ships reduce the effective cost of distance by hauling more per trip and going faster. For example, in Anno 1800, upgrading from a schooner (small sailing ship) to a cargo steamer multiplies your cargo per trip and also removes wind slowdowns, hugely boosting trade efficiency . We want a similar feel: when the player unlocks the Freighter, those inter-island routes that took five trips now take one, but with a catch: a single large shipment can cause bursts of supply that require adequate storage and docking capacity. Bigger ships don’t delete logistics challenges – they change their nature. The player goes from juggling many small trips to handling fewer but larger deliveries. This is good, because it reduces tedium (fewer total trips to set up) but introduces new planning (e.g. “I need a bigger port storage or half the delivery will sit on the dock”). It also means diminishing returns on speed – even the fastest ship still has to load and unload, so beyond a point you can’t reduce the cycle time further, you can only increase capacity. This ensures there’s always some delay in moving goods, keeping distant colonies feeling distinct (a sugar island on the other side of the map can’t supply your main island instantly; you’ll feel the distance).

We will likely implement a pull-based routing system. Instead of micromanaging every ship’s route in detail, the player can set target import/export amounts and the game’s “dispatcher” will allocate ships accordingly. For example, Island Alpha could request 200 units of grain per minute from any source; the system will then send ships from your grain-producing Island Beta until that rate is met. If Beta can’t keep up, you either see ships queuing (indicating a shortfall) or the quota not being met, which signals the player to adjust production or add another source island. Pull-based logistics mirror real supply chain management and prevent scenarios where ships waste time shipping goods that aren’t needed. Many advanced factory games (with mods or late-game research) do this to simplify the logistics – e.g., Factorio’s logistics network or Satisfactory’s drones will deliver on demand. This feature becomes especially valuable when dealing with multi-output issues or dynamic needs: if one island suddenly needs more of output B, ships can re-route from delivering A to B automatically under the hood. Of course, we’ll need to tutorialize this gradually – perhaps initially the player manually assigns a route or two, and only by Epoch 9 do they get an “auto-balancer” feature unlocked.

Another logistic consideration is loss or efficiency over distance – we might impose a small wastage or time-loss to long routes to simulate difficulty (for example, per tile distance reduces effective throughput by a percent due to spoilage or idle time). However, since we’re already incorporating travel time and loading time as natural friction, an explicit loss may not be needed. Instead, the cost is opportunity: a ship on a long route spends a lot of time traveling (during which its goods are effectively out of the system). This is where Little’s Law again tells us why we need bigger storage: if it takes a ship 10 minutes round-trip to bring iron from Island C, Island A needs a buffer of >10 minutes worth of iron consumption to not run dry between shipments. Designing default storage sizes to roughly cover one or two ship trips worth of goods will help players not inadvertently starve their factories. We may communicate this with tooltips like “Your iron smelter will consume 5/min – storage can hold 50, which is 10 minutes of production.” This hints that if the route is longer, upgrade storage or use more ships.

By late game, the logistics layer itself becomes a source of fun and challenge. Players will be effectively designing a global supply network. They’ll be optimizing shipping lanes (maybe choosing shorter routes for high-demand goods, and using the fastest ships for the longest routes), deciding on island specializations (which mirrors how in Anno you might dedicate one island to beer production, another to steel, etc.), and possibly dealing with random events (if we choose to add any, e.g. a storm slows down ships occasionally, or a port gets congested). We can introduce upgrades to aid logistics, like better port loading equipment (reduces dock time), or containerization tech (which might unlock the big container ship and also reduce how storage is handled). These give the player goals to continuously improve their network throughput, which is satisfying as numbers go up and bottlenecks disappear.

It’s worth noting that many players find the logistics puzzles the most rewarding in this genre – setting up a route that perfectly balances supply and demand is like solving a complex equation. We should provide visual feedback for this: graphs or indicators of supply vs consumption on each island, alerts if an island is lacking something or overstocked, etc. A statistics screen by Epoch 9 could show per-minute production and consumption of each resource globally , so hardcore players can fine-tune. More casual players can get by with simpler cues (like an icon over an island if it’s missing a critical good).

Late-Game Systems and Final Challenges

As we move into the late epochs (8, 9, 10), several overarching systems ensure the game remains engaging and doesn’t tip into either trivial automation or unbearable micromanagement. We’ve touched on power and automation earlier, but let’s summarize their roles:
	•	Power Grid: Introduced locally in Epoch 7 and globally connected by Epoch 8, power serves as a global modifier and cap. Running industry on electrical power could yield a multiplier to output (e.g. +20% production speed) to simulate modern efficiency, but the grid capacity is limited, forcing the player to allocate power where it’s most needed. This creates a late-game decision layer: do you power the mines or the factories, or build more generators? Power plants themselves consume fuel (coal, oil, or even uranium if we go nuclear), adding continuous resource demand (a sink) that scales with your industrial size. This is a classic design seen in games like SimCity (power availability limits city growth) and many factory games (Factorio’s factory can’t expand without expanding power, often a separate challenge). By Epoch 8–9, players might have different power sources (wind, solar, diesel, nuclear) with trade-offs, but total output is always a constraint until they invest heavily. This prevents a late-game runaway scenario where the player could just multiply all outputs without bounds – everything is tied back to the power you can generate and maintain. It also spatially differentiates islands: one island might house a big power plant complex (perhaps near an oil field or dam) and transmit energy to others if we allow inter-island power transfer (or each island manages its own grid – that detail can be decided based on technical simplicity). Either way, electricity becomes the “blood” of the late-game economy, and managing it is both a bottleneck and a way to boost production strategically.
	•	Automation & Controls: By the time complex electronics and computers are available (late Epoch 9), it’s reasonable to give the player tools to automate certain tedious tasks. We’ve mentioned auto-balancing logistics. We can also introduce advanced building controls, such as modules or upgrades that let a factory automatically adjust its output rate or toggle recipes based on supply. For example, a refinery could automatically change its oil-to-fuel ratio if plastic is oversupplied. We could have an “AI dispatch” for ships that prioritizes shipments to the most under-supplied island. Essentially, the late game should feel like the player is managing managers – you’re not clicking every little thing, you’re setting policies and watching your system run, intervening only to fix or improve things. This is important because by Epoch 10 the sheer scale (potentially dozens of islands and hundreds of production buildings) would be overwhelming to micromanage. Good late-game design in idle/management games hands more control to the game systems themselves, reflecting the theme (you’ve built an automated society). A real-world parallel is how late-game Anno 1800 allows use of trade unions, items, and commuter piers that simplify management across islands. We’ll do similar by letting players network islands (maybe sharing workforce or power) and set high-level distribution orders instead of shipping each good manually.
	•	Continuous Challenges (No “Dead Grind”): We want to avoid a scenario where after setting everything up, the player just waits idly for a big number to accumulate (a “dead grind”). The rocket assembly design addresses this by requiring active maintenance of output. We can further ensure that by perhaps adding ongoing consumption needs during the final push. For instance, life support systems for the rocket might need a stockpile of food, water, oxygen – and those could slowly decay or be consumed as the launch preps continue, so the player must keep producing until the moment of launch. Another idea is to include a “launch window” timing element: the rocket can only launch when ready and if all parts are finished within, say, a certain timeframe of each other (otherwise you have to run tests again on an older part). This softly pressures the player to have a robust, balanced production rather than doing one part, then the next sequentially with everything else idle. It’s akin to how Factorio encourages building a base that can continuously produce science packs for the rocket, rather than just one big batch – many players try to launch a rocket per minute, which is an endless challenge if they choose . In our game, one rocket is the win condition, but we can offer post-launch play or prestige to extend the challenge (more on that next).
	•	Prestige/Reset Mechanic: Many idle games include a prestige system (e.g. launch the rocket to start over with some permanent bonuses, implying multiple playthroughs). Given the depth of this game, we might not need a full reset; however, a New Game Plus or sandbox mode after winning could be enticing. Alternatively, we integrate prestige within the game fiction: maybe every test launch (like suborbital tests in Epoch 10) yields “Telemetry Data” which is a meta-resource that can be spent on global upgrades (like +5% transport speed, +10% power efficiency, etc.). This way, players who want to optimize further can intentionally do trial launches (costing some resources each time, acting as a sink) to earn buffs that make their economy even smoother. This idea was suggested to avoid extremely surgical balancing at the very late game – if something is a bit grindy, prestige can alleviate it by giving players an option to accelerate progress at the cost of essentially resetting some aspect . We would probably unlock the prestige option only after a certain point (no earlier than Epoch 6 or so, because it wouldn’t make thematic sense to “restart” in the Stone Age too soon). The presence of a prestige layer provides a long-term retention hook and a safety valve for pacing: if a player feels stuck, they might choose to launch an early rocket (maybe a smaller test rocket) to gain a boost on the next run.
	•	Difficulty and Fail States: Notably, the current plan doesn’t include fail states like disasters or enemy attacks (which is fine – many idle/city builders are purely sandboxes you can’t lose). We might want at least some challenge beyond economics, such as occasional events (droughts affecting one island’s farms, or a temporary power plant breakdown) to nudge the player into reacting. But these should be optional or gentle; the main “enemy” in this game is inefficiency. If players neglect something (e.g. forget to supply fuel to a power plant), the feedback loop is that production slows or stops, which is penalty enough (progress stalls until fixed). We should be cautious adding anything that could wipe out progress (no random fire that burns down half the city without player control, for example). The emphasis is on optimization and expansion, not survival against RNG events. This keeps it idle-friendly and low-stress, which is important for retention.

Idle & Mobile-Friendly Considerations

Since this game targets mobile (iOS) and is semi-idle, we must tailor the design to short play sessions and offline progression constraints. The session design should follow an appointment dynamic: early on, sessions might be a few minutes (build something, come back in 5 minutes to collect), and later stretch to longer (come back in a few hours to ship goods around, etc.). We want to align with common idle game loops: e.g. a 4-minute quick check loop, a ~15 minute expansion loop, a ~1 hour mid-term goal, and an overnight 8-hour idle collection loop. These “appointment” durations are common in successful idle and F2P games . To support this, storage caps and build timers will likely be set such that: in 5 minutes your storage fills for early resources (prompting a quick return early on), but by mid-game, storage upgrades allow several hours of offline gains so the player can sleep or work and come back without wastage. For example, if a silo holds 1,000 units and your island produces 100/min, it fills in ~10 minutes at first – but you upgrade it to hold 10,000, now it fills in ~100 minutes, etc. By late game, maybe you have millions of storage and can leave it overnight. The idea is to always give the player meaningful progress when they log back in, but also ensure no progress happens if they neglect upgrades. This was mentioned before: idle gains are capped by storage only, not an arbitrary time limit. It’s straightforward: if you want more offline gains, build more storage or higher-tier storage. This cap forms a gentle pressure to come back and use resources or invest them.

User interface must remain clean despite the complexity under the hood. Progressive disclosure of resources and buildings was discussed; similarly, menus can evolve with epochs (e.g. a power management screen appears only when power is unlocked, a trade routes screen appears only after Epoch 6). Tooltips and glossary can help teach as we go. We should keep paragraphs of explanation out of the way unless the player asks (tutorial steps and contextual tips are fine). Mobile UI also means big buttons, simple layouts – the current technical design using SwiftUI can accommodate switching views per island, etc., but we should ensure that by late game, the player isn’t drowning in icons. Collapsible categories (like grouping resources by type) and filters (show only resources of a certain epoch) can help.

One special consideration is screen real estate for the world map vs island view – when the player reaches Epoch 6, they need an overview of all islands. A possible UI is a world map with icons for islands showing if they have issues (like an exclamation if an island is low on something). The player can largely manage logistics from that view by connecting islands rather than having to tap each island constantly. We want to minimize repetitive actions – e.g. if bread is needed on 3 islands, the player should set up a route once or copy it, instead of manually 3 times.

Monetization / Retention (if applicable): While not explicitly asked, if this is F2P mobile, our design naturally integrates some monetization points without hurting gameplay. Speed-ups could apply to build timers or shipping times (common in builder games), resource bundles could be sold (though careful, because it’s single-player; maybe cosmetic or minor boosts are better). However, given the depth, this might be a premium or single-purchase game, which is fine too. Regardless, our focus is on fair design: exponential costs and idle pacing already encourage the player to take breaks (which is healthy for F2P monetization as well, as it can entice impatience spending, but we ensure the game is playable without it by balancing those exponents reasonably).

In summary, the resource system will evolve through progressive epochs that each highlight a core concept (from basic production to complex logistics), while always tying back into an integrated economy. By using proven design techniques from other semi-idle and city-builder games – such as Anno’s tier-based complexity gating , Factorio’s use of a final rocket as an aggregate goal and resource sink , and idle games’ reliance on carefully tuned growth curves – we can create a coherent experience. The player starts with a stone-age village and ends with a space program, and at each step they learn a new skill: managing storage in E1–E2, balancing production ratios in E3–E4, planning infrastructure in E5, orchestrating trade routes in E6–E8, optimizing power and automation in E9, and finally sustaining a fully integrated economy to achieve the rocket in E10. The arc not only provides the “Anno-style planning satisfaction” and idle-friendly progression as envisioned, but also ensures the economy stays alive and engaging until the very end, rather than degrading into a pure waiting game. By thoroughly thinking through these mechanics and balancing levers now, we set the stage for a game that is deep, rewarding, and manageable in scope – avoiding the pitfalls of unbridled complexity or resource bloat that have sunk similar ambitious projects in the past.

Epoch Agreements:
Here’s one clear epoch-gate research for each hop, with tight descriptions and zero fluff about upkeep or side goals.

Epoch 1: Age of Hunters & Gatherers

E1 → E2 — Agriculture
Formalizes planned production instead of ad-hoc gathering. Unlocks Farm Districts and basic workshops, introduces scheduled cycles, and expands storage so early chains run predictably.

Epoch 2: Age of Agriculture

E2 → E3 — Metallurgy
Transitions from handcraft to heat-based industry. Unlocks Foundry/Smelter class buildings, adds higher-throughput processing tiers, and introduces efficiency upgrades tied to facility levels.

Epoch 3: Age of Iron

E3 → E4 — Extraction & Fabrication
Scales operations from tools to terrain. Unlocks Extractor Hubs/Quarries and a Fabrication Hall, with longer production cycles and larger buffers that make planning matter.

Epoch 4: Age of Knights

E4 → E5 — Optics & Infrastructure
Brings precision manufacturing and civic build-out. Unlocks Glassworks/Precision Shops and the next civic tier, plus clean UI for co-product handling without micromanagement.

Epoch 5: Age of Discovery

E5 → E6 — Oceanfaring & Colonization Charter
Opens the world map and cross-island logistics (the only transport in the game). Unlocks Harbor construction, route planner with pull-based quotas, and the Shipyard’s Skiff class for first colonies.

Epoch 6: Age of Colonisation

E6 → E7 — Industrial Standardization
Normalizes heavy industry across multiple islands. Unlocks Heavy Works and Shipyard II with the Cutter class; adds blueprinting and island specialization tools for copy-paste, reliable scaling.

Epoch 7: Age of Industry

E7 → E8 — Grid Engineering & Petrochemistry
Electrifies the economy and formalizes co-product processing. Unlocks Power Plant I, grid visualization with capacity/consumption limits, Refineries, and Harbor II with Freighter class vessels.

Epoch 8: Age of Power

E8 → E9 — Microfabrication & Automation
Shifts the focus from bulk to brains. Unlocks Clean Room manufacturing, an Automation Controller for per-minute quotas and auto-balancing, and Container Ship capability for efficient long routes.

Epoch 9: Age of Automation

E9 → E10 — Space Systems Integration
Converts advanced industry into aerospace. Unlocks space-system assembly lines, specialized handling and test protocols, and end-to-end integration checks that prepare you to build the four rocket subsystems.

Epoch 10: Age of New Frontiers

E10 (final): Launch Authorization
No research gate—this is the win state. Assemble the four subsystems, pass final checks, and launch.
